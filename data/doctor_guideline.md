# Doctor Guideline Care Notes
Updated: 2026-06-04T05:03:57.334Z

This file is not a substitute for medical care. Parent concern, pediatrician guidance, and emergency services come first.

Use these notes for calm daily care, pattern review, and questions to ask the pediatrician. When a baby seems very sick, has breathing trouble, has a concerning fever, has signs of dehydration, or a parent is worried, medical care comes first.

## When To Get Medical Help
### Activity
- Tummy time should be supervised and only while baby is awake.
  - What to do: Start with short sessions and build up gradually; by about 7 weeks, aim for 15 to 30 minutes total per day if tolerated.
  - Source: [Back to Sleep, Tummy to Play](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/back-to-sleep-tummy-to-play.aspx)

### Daily logging
- Daily review is stronger when sleep, feed duration or amount, breast side, pee, poop, and notes are logged together.
  - What to do: Use missing fields as unknown rather than a problem; add notes when the day is unusual.
  - Source: Simplest Baby Plan: Baby Schedules & Log

### Diaper
- Red stool or blood in stool should be discussed with the pediatrician.
  - What to do: Call the pediatrician.
  - Source: [The Many Colors of Baby Poop](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/The-Many-Colors-of-Poop.aspx); [Diarrhea (0-12 Months) Symptom Checker](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/tips-tools/symptom-checker/Pages/symptomviewer.aspx?symptom=Diarrhea+%280-12+Months%29)
- White, pale, or gray stool can signal a bile/liver issue and should be treated as urgent.
  - What to do: Call the pediatrician urgently.
  - Source: [The Many Colors of Baby Poop](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/The-Many-Colors-of-Poop.aspx)
- Black stool is expected meconium in the first few days, but black stool after that may need review.
  - What to do: Call the pediatrician if baby is older than 3 days.
  - Source: [The Many Colors of Baby Poop](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/The-Many-Colors-of-Poop.aspx)

### Feeding
- Many newborns breastfeed about 8 to 12 times in 24 hours.
  - What to do: Use hunger/full cues and discuss concerns about intake or weight with the pediatrician or lactation support.
  - Source: [How Much and How Often to Breastfeed](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/breastfeeding/how-much-and-how-often.html); [Newborn Breastfeeding Basics](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/breastfeeding/newborn-basics.html)
- Many formula-fed newborns feed 8 to 12 times in 24 hours, with larger amounts as they grow.
  - What to do: Follow hunger/full cues and the formula label; ask the baby's doctor about amount if unsure.
  - Source: [How Much and How Often to Feed Infant Formula](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/formula-feeding/how-much-and-how-often.html)
- Solid foods usually begin at about 6 months when developmentally ready; introducing solids before 4 months is not recommended.
  - What to do: Look for readiness signs: sitting with support, good head/neck control, opening mouth for food, swallowing instead of pushing food out, and bringing objects to the mouth.
  - Source: [When, What, and How to Introduce Solid Foods](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/foods-and-drinks/when-what-and-how-to-introduce-solid-foods.html)
- Good latch support includes tummy-to-tummy positioning, waiting for a wide mouth, bringing baby to breast chin-first, and checking that baby nose is not blocked.
  - What to do: If latch or nipple pain persists, ask a lactation consultant or pediatrician for hands-on help.
  - Source: Simplest Baby: Breastfeeding Essentials; [Newborn Breastfeeding Basics](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/breastfeeding/newborn-basics.html)

### Feeding safety
- Freshly expressed breast milk can be stored up to 4 hours at room temperature, up to 4 days in the refrigerator, and about 6 months best in the freezer; up to 12 months is acceptable.
  - What to do: Label milk with date, use oldest milk first, and do not refreeze thawed milk.
  - Source: [Breast Milk Storage and Preparation](https://www.cdc.gov/breastfeeding/breast-milk-preparation-and-storage/handling-breastmilk.html)
- Most powdered formula can be prepared by following the manufacturer's instructions with safe tap water, unless a baby has special risk factors or local water is unsafe.
  - What to do: Follow the formula label exactly and use safe water. Ask the pediatrician for higher-risk infants.
  - Source: [Infant Formula Preparation and Storage](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/formula-feeding/preparation-and-storage.html)
- Round, firm, sticky, or hard foods can be choking hazards for babies and young children.
  - What to do: Avoid or modify choking-risk foods; cut foods safely and supervise eating.
  - Source: [Choking Prevention for Babies & Children](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/health-issues/injuries-emergencies/Pages/Choking-Prevention.aspx)

### Growth
- For children under 2 years, use WHO growth standard charts rather than CDC growth charts.
  - What to do: Calculate weight-for-age, length-for-age, weight-for-length, and head-circumference-for-age using WHO standards when valid measurements are available.
  - Source: [What Growth Charts Are Recommended?](https://www.cdc.gov/growth-chart-training/hcp/overview/recommended.html)

### Hydration
- No urine for more than 8 hours, dark urine with very dry mouth, or no tears can suggest dehydration.
  - What to do: Call the pediatrician or seek care now, especially with fever, vomiting, diarrhea, or poor feeding.
  - Source: [Diarrhea (0-12 Months) Symptom Checker](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/tips-tools/symptom-checker/Pages/symptomviewer.aspx?symptom=Diarrhea+%280-12+Months%29)
- By about day 5, fewer than expected wet diapers can mean feeding/output needs review.
  - What to do: Call the pediatrician or lactation support, especially if feeding is poor or baby is sleepy/lethargic.
  - Source: [Newborn Breastfeeding Basics](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/breastfeeding/newborn-basics.html)

### Milestones
- Developmental milestones are age-based skills children commonly reach in how they play, learn, speak, act, and move.
  - What to do: Treat unrecorded milestones as unknown, not failed. Share concerns with the pediatrician.
  - Source: [CDC's Developmental Milestones](https://www.cdc.gov/act-early/milestones/index.html)

### Nutrition
- Babies fed only breast milk or both breast milk and formula generally need 400 IU of vitamin D daily beginning shortly after birth.
  - What to do: Use the pediatrician-recommended vitamin D plan and log supplements if helpful.
  - Source: [Vitamin D](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/vitamins-minerals/vitamin-d.html)

### Safety
- Severe breathing trouble, stopped breathing, passing out, or bluish lips/face needs emergency care.
  - What to do: Call 911 now.
  - Source: [Breathing Trouble Symptom Checker](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/tips-tools/symptom-checker/Pages/symptomviewer.aspx?symptom=Breathing+Trouble)

### Sleep
- The safest infant sleep setup is back sleeping on a firm, flat, non-inclined surface with no loose bedding or soft objects.
  - What to do: Use a crib, bassinet, portable crib, or play yard that meets safety standards. Keep pillows, blankets, bumpers, and stuffed toys out of the sleep area.
  - Source: [How to Keep Your Sleeping Baby Safe: AAP Policy Explained](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/a-parents-guide-to-safe-sleep.aspx); [9 Ways to Reduce a Baby's Risk of SIDS & Suffocation](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/Preventing-SIDS.aspx)
- Side/stomach sleeping, inclined sleep surfaces, loose bedding, or soft objects increase sleep safety risk.
  - What to do: Place baby on the back on a firm, flat sleep surface with no loose bedding or soft objects.
  - Source: [How to Keep Your Sleeping Baby Safe: AAP Policy Explained](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/a-parents-guide-to-safe-sleep.aspx); [9 Ways to Reduce a Baby's Risk of SIDS & Suffocation](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/Preventing-SIDS.aspx)
- A predictable bedtime routine, dim nighttime care, white noise if helpful, and a comfortable sleep room can support settling.
  - What to do: Use these as gentle routine tools while keeping all AAP safe sleep rules in place.
  - Source: Simplest Baby: Sleeping Essentials
- Swaddling should stop once baby begins to roll over.
  - What to do: Switch to an age-appropriate sleep sack or other safe sleep clothing and continue back sleeping on a firm, flat surface.
  - Source: Simplest Baby: Sleeping Essentials; [How to Keep Your Sleeping Baby Safe: AAP Policy Explained](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/a-parents-guide-to-safe-sleep.aspx)
- A swaddle that is too loose can cover baby face; a swaddle that is too tight around the hips can be unsafe.
  - What to do: Use a snug but hip-safe swaddle only for back sleep, and stop swaddling when rolling begins.
  - Source: Simplest Baby: Sleeping Essentials

### Temperature
- A rectal temperature of 100.4°F / 38°C or higher in a baby under 12 weeks needs prompt medical care.
  - What to do: Seek medical care now. Do not give fever medicine before the baby is seen unless directed by a clinician.
  - Source: [Fever and Your Baby](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/health-issues/conditions/fever/Pages/Fever-and-Your-Baby.aspx); [Urgent Care, ER or Pediatrician? A Parent Guide](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/family-life/health-management/Pages/urgent-care-ER-or-pediatrician-a-parent-guide.aspx)
- A non-rectal temperature reading can be less reliable in young infants, but this is still worth prompt pediatric guidance.
  - What to do: Contact the pediatrician for next steps. If baby looks very ill or has breathing trouble, seek urgent care.
  - Source: [Fever and Your Baby](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/health-issues/conditions/fever/Pages/Fever-and-Your-Baby.aspx)

## Trusted Medical Sources
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: Fever and Your Baby](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/health-issues/conditions/fever/Pages/Fever-and-Your-Baby.aspx)
  - Rectal temperature is the most reliable infant temperature method.
  - A rectal temperature of 100.4 F / 38 C or higher in babies 3 months or younger needs immediate pediatrician contact.
  - Environmental heat is not the same as infectious fever; heatstroke concerns require urgent care.
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: Urgent Care, ER or Pediatrician? A Parent Guide](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/family-life/health-management/Pages/urgent-care-ER-or-pediatrician-a-parent-guide.aspx)
  - Helps parents decide between pediatrician, urgent care, emergency department, or 911 based on symptoms.
  - Fever under 3 months and breathing concerns should be treated as higher-priority thresholds.
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: Breathing Trouble Symptom Checker](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/tips-tools/symptom-checker/Pages/symptomviewer.aspx?symptom=Breathing+Trouble)
  - Severe trouble breathing, stopped breathing, passing out, or bluish lips/face are emergency warning signs.
  - Breathing guidance should be used only when matching symptoms are logged or reported.
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: How to Keep Your Sleeping Baby Safe: AAP Policy Explained](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/a-parents-guide-to-safe-sleep.aspx)
  - Place babies on their backs for sleep.
  - Use a firm, flat, non-inclined sleep surface.
  - Keep pillows, blankets, stuffed toys, bumper pads, and loose bedding out of the sleep space.
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: 9 Ways to Reduce a Baby's Risk of SIDS & Suffocation](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/Preventing-SIDS.aspx)
  - Use back sleep and a clear sleep space to reduce sleep-related risk.
  - Avoid soft objects and loose bedding in the sleep area.
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: The Many Colors of Baby Poop](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/The-Many-Colors-of-Poop.aspx)
  - Yellow, brown, and green stools are often common after meconium clears.
  - Red/bloody stool should be discussed with the pediatrician.
  - White, pale, or gray stool needs prompt doctor attention.
  - Black stool after the meconium period is worth pediatrician review.
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: Diarrhea (0-12 Months) Symptom Checker](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/tips-tools/symptom-checker/Pages/symptomviewer.aspx?symptom=Diarrhea+%280-12+Months%29)
  - No urine for 8 hours, very dark urine, dry mouth, no tears, blood in stool, or concerning vomiting can be dehydration or illness flags.
  - Use diaper output trends as review support, not diagnosis.
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: Back to Sleep, Tummy to Play](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/back-to-sleep-tummy-to-play.aspx)
  - Back to sleep remains the sleep rule.
  - Supervised tummy time while awake supports strength and motor practice.
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: Sleep](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/default.aspx)
  - Sleep patterns change with age; tracking start and wake times helps estimate sleep duration.
  - Sleep guidance should stay compatible with AAP safe sleep guidance.
- [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: How Much and How Often to Breastfeed](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/breastfeeding/how-much-and-how-often.html)
  - Newborns may breastfeed every 1 to 3 hours in the first days.
  - Most breastfed babies feed about 8 to 12 times in 24 hours.
  - Feeding needs vary; cues and pediatrician guidance matter.
- [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: Newborn Breastfeeding Basics](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/breastfeeding/newborn-basics.html)
  - Early breastfeeding support can help with latch, milk transfer, and feeding concerns.
  - Parents should contact a clinician or lactation support if feeding concerns persist.
- [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: How Much and How Often to Feed Infant Formula](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/formula-feeding/how-much-and-how-often.html)
  - Formula amount and timing vary by age and baby cues.
  - Use bottle logs as context rather than a diagnosis of intake adequacy.
- [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: Breast Milk Storage and Preparation](https://www.cdc.gov/breastfeeding/breast-milk-preparation-and-storage/handling-breastmilk.html)
  - Freshly expressed breast milk can generally be kept up to 4 hours at room temperature, 4 days refrigerated, and about 6 months best in the freezer.
  - Use clean storage containers and safe thawing/warming practices.
- [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: Infant Formula Preparation and Storage](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/formula-feeding/preparation-and-storage.html)
  - Follow label instructions for mixing formula and use safe water/prep practices.
  - Prepared formula storage and discard timing matter for safety.
- [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: Vitamin D](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/vitamins-minerals/vitamin-d.html)
  - Breastfed or partially breastfed infants generally need 400 IU vitamin D daily shortly after birth unless clinician guidance differs.
  - Formula intake can affect whether extra vitamin D is needed; ask the clinician if unsure.
- [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: When, What, and How to Introduce Solid Foods](https://www.cdc.gov/infant-toddler-nutrition/foods-and-drinks/when-what-and-how-to-introduce-solid-foods.html)
  - Introduce foods other than breast milk or formula at about 6 months; before 4 months is not recommended.
  - Readiness signs include sitting with support, head/neck control, opening mouth for food, and swallowing rather than pushing food out.
  - Try single-ingredient foods one at a time at first.
- [American Academy of Pediatrics / HealthyChildren.org: Choking Prevention for Babies & Children](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/health-issues/injuries-emergencies/Pages/Choking-Prevention.aspx)
  - Modify or avoid high-risk choking foods and supervise eating.
  - Food size, texture, and baby readiness matter for safety.
- [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: What Growth Charts Are Recommended?](https://www.cdc.gov/growth-chart-training/hcp/overview/recommended.html)
  - CDC recommends WHO Child Growth Standards for U.S. children from birth to 2 years.
  - At age 2 years, CDC recommends transitioning to CDC Growth Charts.
  - Accurate measurement and recording are essential for meaningful growth tracking.
- [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: CDC's Developmental Milestones](https://www.cdc.gov/act-early/milestones/index.html)
  - CDC milestones are age-based developmental tracking tools.
  - Unrecorded milestones mean not recorded yet, not failed.
  - Developmental concerns should be discussed with the pediatrician.

## Parent-Provided PDF Notes
These parent-provided PDFs are useful for routines, logging, sleep setup, and breastfeeding technique. For medical decisions, use pediatrician guidance and the trusted medical sources above.

### Simplest Baby Plan: Baby Schedules & Log
- Daily logs should capture start/end time, total sleep, total feed time, feeding amount, breast started with, pee, poop, and notes.
- Sample day schedules are examples for 3-6 weeks, 3-6 months, and 6-12 months; use them as rhythm context, not rigid rules.
- Daytime activity, sleep, feeding, diaper output, and notes belong in one review view because day patterns affect night patterns.

### Simplest Baby: Sleeping Essentials
- Sleep tips include a consistent bedtime routine, dark room, white noise, limited eye contact during nighttime care, room temperature around 68-72 F, and an optional final feeding before bed.
- SIDS-risk reminders include firm crib mattress, back sleeping, no stomach or side sleeping, no bumpers, no blankets or plush toys in the crib, no smoking around baby, and avoiding overheating.
- Swaddling and swaddle alternatives are only for back sleeping; stop swaddling once baby begins to roll over. Avoid swaddles that are too loose or too tight around the hips.

### Simplest Baby: Breastfeeding Essentials
- Latch guidance emphasizes tummy-to-tummy positioning, tickling the upper lip with the nipple, waiting for a wide mouth, aiming the nipple toward the roof of the mouth, and checking that the nose is not blocked.
- Breastfeeding positions shown include cradle, cross-cradle, side-lying, and football hold.
- Milk-production support notes include nursing or pumping often, staying hydrated, eating well, and considering clinician-approved nutrition or probiotic guidance.
- Sore-breast comfort tips include brief moist heat before feeding, cold compresses after feeding, gentle massage/compression while baby pauses, supportive nursing bra, hand expression or brief pumping to soften the areola, and asking a health-care professional before medication.

### Breastfeeding Essentials PDF Notes
#### Getting a Good Latch
1. Position baby directly facing parent, tummy against parent, and slowly bring baby chin-first toward the breast.
2. Place fingers around the areola and bring baby to the breast; tickle the upper lip with the nipple to encourage a wide mouth.
3. When baby opens wide with chin down, cup baby gently and bring baby onto the breast with the nipple angled toward the roof of the mouth.
4. Roll the breast fully into the mouth so baby takes the nipple and at least 1.5 inches of areola; check that the nose is not blocked.

#### Breastfeeding Positions
- Cradle hold: common everyday breastfeeding position.
- Cross-cradle hold: helpful for newborns or young babies needing more support.
- Side-lying hold: can be relaxing for parent and baby when used safely while awake.
- Football hold: may be more comfortable during recovery or when parent wants side support.

#### Milk Production Support
- Nurse or pump often to stimulate milk production.
- Stay well hydrated.
- Eat enough food; the PDF mentions oatmeal, flax seed, brewer yeast, and omega-3-rich foods as examples.
- Ask a clinician or lactation consultant before using supplements or medication for supply concerns.

#### Sore Breast Comfort
- Use moist heat briefly before breastfeeding or shower before feeding to encourage milk flow; avoid extended heat if swelling worsens.
- Use cold compresses for about 10 minutes after feeding to reduce swelling.
- Gently massage and compress the breast when baby pauses between sucks to help drain milk.
- Consider a well-fitted supportive nursing bra.
- Use hand expression or brief pumping to soften the nipple and areola so baby can latch better.
- Ask a health-care professional before using pain or inflammation medication.

### Outdoor Clothing by Temperature
- Up to 45 F: Bundle warmly: footed layer, warm outer layer, hat, mittens, blanket; limit exposure.
- Up to 55 F: Warm layer + jacket or bunting, hat, socks; check neck/chest for warmth.
- Up to 67 F: Long sleeves/pants plus light jacket or blanket.
- Up to 74 F: Comfortable light layer; bring a thin blanket.
- Up to 84 F: Light breathable clothes, sun shade, and hydration breaks.
- Up to 100 F: Very light clothing, shade, avoid direct sun/heat, watch for overheating.

### Indoor Clothing
Aim for a comfortable sleep room, often around 68-72 F if practical; check baby chest/neck and avoid overheating.

### Sleeping Essentials PDF Notes
#### Good Night Sleep Tips
- Use a consistent bedtime routine.
- Keep nighttime care dark or use a dimmer during feeds and changes.
- Use white noise if it helps baby settle.
- Limit eye contact during night feeds and changes so baby stays sleepy.
- Keep the sleep room comfortable, around 68-72 F when practical.
- If breastfeeding, consider parent caffeine intake because caffeine can affect baby.
- A gentle 15-minute bedtime massage may help some babies relax.
- Offer one last feeding before bed if baby is showing hunger cues.

#### SIDS Risk Reduction Reminders
- Use a firm crib mattress.
- Put baby to sleep on the back, never on the stomach or side.
- Do not use crib bumpers, blankets, plush toys, or loose bedding in the crib.
- Do not smoke around baby.
- Avoid overheating and keep room temperature comfortable.
- Keep vaccines up to date with the pediatrician schedule.

#### Swaddling Basics and Cautions
- Swaddling is only for back sleeping.
- Stop swaddling once baby begins to roll over.
- Avoid swaddles that are too loose because loose fabric can cover baby face.
- Avoid swaddles that are too tight around baby hips.
- Swaddle alternatives shown include swaddle-up blankets, sleep sacks, and sleep suits; choose options that still allow safe back sleep and age-appropriate movement.

## Milestones and Play
### Milestone Ideas to Watch For
- First Smile (6–12 weeks)
  - Helpful play: Face-to-face talking, Exaggerated smiles, Singing, Eye contact during feeding, Mimic baby's facial expressions
- Reaching & Grabbing (3–5 months)
  - Helpful play: Dangling toys, Soft rattles, Play gym, Bring toys to midline, Hand-to-hand toy transfer practice
- Rolling Over (4–6 months)
  - Helpful play: Daily tummy time, Place toys slightly to the side, Encourage reaching across the body, Side-lying play
- Army Crawling / Scooting (5–8 months)
  - Helpful play: Extended tummy time, Place favorite toy just out of reach, Mirror play, Encourage weight shifting on forearms
- Sitting Up (7–9 months)
  - Helpful play: Supported sitting, Sit between parent's legs, Place toys around baby to encourage balance, Tripod sitting hands forward
- Crawling (6–10 months)
  - Helpful play: Tunnel games, Toy just out of reach, Obstacle pillows, Encourage hands-and-knees rocking
- First Syllables ("ba", "ma", "da") (9–11 months)
  - Helpful play: Narrate daily activities, Read books, Imitate sounds, Sing songs, Pause and wait for baby to respond
- Walking (9–15 months)
  - Helpful play: Cruise along furniture, Push toys, Stand-and-reach games, Squat-and-stand play, Barefoot time indoors
- Tracks faces (1–2 months)
- Hands to mouth (2–4 months)
- Responds to name (6–9 months)
- Transfers toy hand-to-hand (5–7 months)
- Peek-a-boo understanding (7–10 months)
- Waves bye-bye (8–12 months)
- Claps hands (8–12 months)
- Finger feeding (8–12 months)
- Drinks from cup with help (6–12 months)
- Points with finger (9–14 months)
- Eats solids (4+ months)

### Exercise and Play Library
#### Tummy Time
- Purpose: Strengthens baby's neck, shoulder, back, and core muscles; Helps prepare for rolling, sitting, army crawling, and crawling
- Timing: Start right after birth; Continue until baby can sit up well
- Recommended Amount: Newborn: a few minutes, a couple times per day; 2 months old: 10–15 minute sessions, total about 1 hour per day; 3 months old: 10–15 minute sessions, total about 1.5 hours per day
- Safety: Do not do right after feeding; Always supervise; Stop if baby is very upset
- Methods: Traditional tummy time: place baby on stomach on a safe flat surface; Pillow tummy time: place baby over a U-shaped pillow, rolled blanket, or towel; Tummy-to-tummy: parent reclines and baby lies on parent's chest
- Supports Milestones: Rolling Over; Army Crawling / Scooting; Sitting Up; Crawling

#### Baby Sit-ups
- Purpose: Helps build neck strength, core strength, and trunk control
- Safety: Only do this when baby has good head control; Pull gently; Never yank baby's arms; Stop if baby looks uncomfortable
- Methods: Baby lies on back; Gently hold baby's hands or arms; Slowly pull baby upward; Slowly lower baby down; Pause when almost at the bottom
- Supports Milestones: Sitting Up; Crawling

#### Baby Floor Gym
- Purpose: Supports visual tracking, reaching, grabbing, and hand-eye coordination
- Methods: Let baby look at dangling toys; Encourage baby to reach for toys; Encourage baby to kick hanging toys; Practice hand-to-hand toy transfer later
- Supports Milestones: Reaching & Grabbing; Tracks faces; Hands to mouth; Transfers toy hand-to-hand
